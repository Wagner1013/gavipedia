Prison And Hospital Reforms

Description

At the beginning of the 1800s, prisons were overcrowded and unsanitary and people with mental illnesses were mistreated and harmed. Children and adults were being jailed together, while people with mental illnesses were chained up and locked in cages. Prison and hospital reform was necessary for troubled people to become members of society.

Dates of Activity

People began to notice the poor treatment of inmates around the 1820s. Throughout the 19th century, reformers worked to improve the life of prisoners so that they are safe but still serving time in prison. On the mental illness side, people noticed the poor treatment of the mentally ill in the early 1800s. Reformers worked through the 1880s to help create mental institutions where the mentally ill are cared for.

Important Leaders

Dorthea Dix was an activist that spoke out about the mistreatment of prisoners, especially the mistreatment of the mentally ill. She petitioned for better treatment of prisoners and taught Sunday School at prisons. She also was able to convince people that it was not wrong to be mentally ill. She helped improve the lives of many people.

Reforms Sought Out

Prison reformers wanted to make sure that common problems such as overcrowding, lack of food, and abuse were being fixed. Prison reformers thought that even though prisoners had broken the law, they should still be treated like humans. Hospital and mental health reformers wanted to make sure that people who suffered from mental illnesses weren’t being chained up, neglected, and beaten just because they may not be fully mentally capable. They wanted the mentally ill to be cared for and have access to places like mental institutions where they can get help for their conditions.

Significant Dates

  • 1816 - Auburn Prison is built and reflects a new kind of treatment in prison.
  • 1829 - The Eastern State Penitentiary is built and continues the change in prisoner treatment.
  • 1838 - Dorthea Dix begins her crusade against the poor conditions of the mentally ill.
  • 1847 - Massachusetts is the first state to stop sentencing people to death.
  • 1865-1880 - More than 25 mental institutions are established, providing a safer place for the mentally ill to reside.

Legislation Changes

The Prison reformers were able to get Massachusetts to outlaw the death penalty in 1847. Rhode Island followed by outlawing the death penalty in 1852. There weren’t many more laws that changed the prison system, however, the construction of two new prisons, the Auburn Prison and the Eastern State Penitentiary, led to a change in the prison system. Massachusetts led the charge again by creating legislation to help people with mental illness. A man named Reverend Louis Dwight started an organization called Boston Prison Discipline Society in 1825 that pushed for the creation of these laws. This organization worked to improve the conditions of prisoners and the mentally ill and had a great impact.

Summary and Impact

Thanks to the work of prison and hospital reformers, the lives of America’s inmates and the mentally ill were greatly improved in the 1800s. Instead of being packed together and abused in our prison system, inmates now have an environment where they can reflect on their mistakes and become better members of society. The work of reformers for the benefit of the mentally ill was a huge success in the sense that people with those issues are no longer abused and chained up. Instead, they are helped and cared for by workers in places like mental institutions.
