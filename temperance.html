The Temperance Movement

Description

In the 1800s, alcohol abuse was widespread across the United States and it was leading to the separation of families, crime, and mental illness. The Temperance Movement was created to stop the sale and consumption of alcohol.

Dates of Activity

The Temperance movement was active from the early 1800s to the repealment of the 18th Amendment (National Prohibition Act) in 1933.

Important Leaders

Carry Nation was an activist for the Temperance Movement. When she was young, she married a physician named Charles Glyod; however, she divorced him a few months later due to his severe alcoholism. She joined the Temperance Movement in 1890 after the Supreme Court ruled in favor of liquor sales. She earned the nickname “Hatchet Granny” because she would go into places of liquor sales and smash things.

Significant Dates

  • 1808 - The first Temperance organization was created in Saratoga, New York.
  • 1833 - There are more than 6,000 local temperance organizations in The United States.
  • 1842 - Over one million people in the Washingtonian Temperance Society are pledged to stay away from alcohol.
  • 1851 - Maine is the first state in the Union to ban the production and sale of alcohol.
  • 1851-1855 - 12 states banned the manufacturing and sale of liquor.

Legislation Changes

The Temperance Movement sought the ban of the movement, sale, and manufacturing of liquor in the US. They achieved this goal in 1919 when the 18th Amendment was passed and banned all of these actions. However, the 18th Amendment was repealed in 1933 allowing the production and distribution of liquor to continue.

Summary and Impact

In the end, the Temperance Movement brought awareness to the fact that alcohol abuse was a big problem in society. Even though the movement wasn’t successful in sustaining a nationwide ban on alcohol, it led to the creation of programs like AA and SADD that help people reform their lives today.
