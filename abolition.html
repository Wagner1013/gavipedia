The Abolition Movement

Description

The Abolition Movement was a group of people who pushed for the total freedom and immediate emancipation of enslaved people in America. The Abolition Movement also believed that slave owners shouldn’t have to be paid or reimbursed for their lost slaves which they considered property.

Dates of Activity

The Abolition Movement started in the late 1700s and ended when slavery became illegal and all slaves were set free in the year 1865.

Important Leaders

William Lloyd Garrison was a white man from the north. In Boston, he founded a newspaper called The Liberator in 1831. The newspaper contained anti-slavery views and became one of the most influential abolitionist media outputs in the United States.

Frederick Douglass was a slave until he escaped at the age of 20 in 1838. After Douglass reached freedom in the north, he helped other slaves escape their cruel masters. Douglass also published a newspaper called The North Star which shared anti-slavery ideas similar to what The Liberator was doing. In 1845, Douglass published his autobiography called Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave. Frederick Douglass gave speeches about the cruelty of slavery and became a notable leader of the movement.

Harriet Tubman, a former slave who had escaped her owner, was a conductor on the Underground Railroad. The Underground Railroad was a system of abolitionists who helped get people out of slavery and up into the north where they would be free. Tubman helped over 300 people escape slavery.

Reforms Sought Out

The Abolition Movement sought complete freedom for all enslaved people in the United States. They also wanted to make sure that former slave owners didn’t receive any compensation for losing their slaves.

Significant Dates

  • 1804 - All states north of Maryland have abolished slavery. Only the southern states still allow slavery.
  • 1831 - William Lloyd Garrison founded his abolition newspaper, The Liberator.
  • 1845 - Frederick Douglass published his autobiography entitled, Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave, which shares his life story and the terrors of slavery.
  • 1863 - The Emancipation Proclamation is signed by President Abraham Lincoln, freeing all slaves in the rebellious states.
  • 1865 - The 13th Amendment is passed making all slavery and involuntary servitude illegal.

Legislation Changes

The Abolition Movement received a big win when President Abraham Lincoln signed the Emancipation Proclamation. This proclamation stated that all slaves in the rebellious states were free. The Emancipation Proclamation led the way for the creation and ratification of the 13th Amendment which officially set all slaves in the United States free and prohibited any further slavery or involuntary servitude.

Summary and Impact

Slavery was a sad part of our nation’s history, but thanks to the bold efforts of the Abolition Movement, slavery is no longer a prominent part of our society. The Abolition Movement helped the United States redefine the phrase “All men are created equal” because at the time this phrase only applied to white men. Now this phrase applies to all people in the United States no matter race, ethnicity, or gender.
