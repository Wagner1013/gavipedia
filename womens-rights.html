The Women's Rights Movement

Description

Before the Women’s Rights movement, women were expected to remain at home and do chores. Women could not go to college or university, they could not own land, and they could not vote. When women started helping fight for the rights of others, many of them realized they did not have rights themselves. The Women’s Rights Movement believed in equality for all regardless of gender.

Dates of Activity

The first organized Women’s Rights movement started in 1848, however, the idea started in 1840 when Lucretia Mott and Elizabeth Cady Stanton were denied the right to speak at the World Anti-Slavery Convention in London. The fight for women’s rights ended around the time the 19th Amendment, allowing women to vote, was ratified.

Important Leaders

Lucretia Mott was an abolitionist who was fighting to free slaves. In 1840, she attended the World Anti-Slavery Convention, however when she tried to speak, she was rejected simply because she was a woman. Upon returning home, she began to campaign for women’s rights, and in 1848, she worked with Elizabeth Cady Stanton and organized the first woman's rights convention in Seneca Falls, New York. Throughout her life, she continued to fight for women’s rights. She was a true pioneer for women’s rights.

Susan B. Anthony, like many other women’s rights activists, started as an abolitionist. She had met with William Lloyd Garrison and Frederick Douglass and was moved by their stories and became a big activist. Giving speeches and fighting for the freedom of slaves. Later, Anthony met Elizabeth Cady Stanton. The two traveled the US delivering speeches on equality even though they risked being arrested for sharing their opinions in public. Anthony later co-founded the American Equal Rights Association and the National Woman Suffrage Association. Susan B. Anthony will be remembered as one of the most influential fighters for rights in US history.

Reforms Sought Out

The Women’s Rights Movement wanted rights like the right to vote, hold public office, and own land to be granted to them. They wanted true equality in the US whether you were male or female.

Significant Dates

  • 1848 - The first woman's rights convention was held in Seneca Falls, New York.
  • 1866 - The American Equal Rights Association is founded.
  • 1869 - The National Woman Suffrage Association is founded.
  • 1890 - Wyoming is the first state to allow women to vote in state elections.
  • 1920 - The 19th Amendment is ratified and women now have the right to vote.

Legislation Changes

The Women’s Rights movement’s first win was when the state of Wyoming became the first state to allow women to vote in state elections in 1890. Later, the 19th Amendment was passed in 1920 giving women the right to vote across the whole country.

Summary and Impact

The Women’s Rights movement was able to reach all of its goals and the reforms they sought after greatly improved equality in America. Thanks to the Women’s Rights Movement, women are now allowed to vote, run for public office, and own land. These brave women helped make America the place of freedom it is today.
